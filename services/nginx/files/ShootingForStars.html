Project Overview

Machine learning meets
ancient science

Astronomy is one of humanity's oldest pursuits. Yet terms like "main sequence," "white dwarf," and "red giant" remain opaque to most. This project bridges that gap as users are able to observe stellar parameters and receive a clear and explained classification.

We can observe or calculate their surface temperature, luminosity, radius, and absolute magnitude, and these are excellent parameters to use for classification. Machine learning has become a way for us to solve this classification task derived from such a feature set. We used two types of machine learning models: a decision tree and a multi-layer perceptron.

Classification

The spectral
types

One way to classify stars is to look at their spectra—the different colors of light that they emit and absorb. This directly corellates to surface temperature and atmospheric composition. The symbols below represents the primary system used to classify stars in this way. This system is known as the Harvard spectral classification.

O
Blue Supergiant
The hottest, most massive stars. Extremely rare, burning through their fuel in mere millions of years.
> 30,000 K
B
Blue-White Giant
Very luminous stars with strong helium absorption lines. Often found in stellar associations, which are a type of star cluster.
10,000 – 30,000 K
A
White Star
Prominent hydrogen Balmer absorption lines. Sirius and Vega are familiar class A stars.
7,500 – 10,000 K
F
Yellow-White Star
Slightly cooler than A-class, showing ionized calcium lines. Polaris belongs here.
6,000 – 7,500 K
G
Yellow Dwarf
Main sequence stars like our Sun. Balanced hydrogen and metal absorption lines.
5,200 – 6,000 K
K
Orange Dwarf
Cooler than the Sun, stable and long-lived. Strong candidates for hosting habitable planets.
3,700 – 5,200 K
M
Red Dwarf
The most common type in the galaxy. Very low mass, long-lived, with molecular absorption bands.
2,400 – 3,700 K
WD
White Dwarf
The dense remnant of a low-to-medium mass star after it dies and sheds its outer layers. Earth-sized but very massive.
8,000 – 40,000 K
NS
Neutron Star
Collapsed core of a massive star after a supernova. Incredibly dense—a teaspoon would weigh billions of tons.
> 106 K (surface)
